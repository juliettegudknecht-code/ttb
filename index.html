What survived Prohibition? The tax.

In 1910 beer was taxed at $1.00 a barrel and spirits at $1.10 a proof gallon. War drove the rates up, and Prohibition outlawed the products without ever repealing the taxes. The ledger never closed.

I

Why did collections fall 19 percent in a decade?

TTB brought in $25.5 billion in FY 2015 and $20.6 billion in FY 2025; tobacco more than accounts for the decline. The import share, collected at the border by U.S. Customs and Border Protection, grew from 13 to 25 percent.

Since 1791

The Whiskey Rebellion. 1791: Alexander Hamilton slaps the young republic's first tax on something made at home, and the frontier does not take kindly to it. Stills vanish into the hills, tax men ride out and come home tarred and feathered, and by 1794 George Washington himself saddles up with the militia to put it down. The rebels folded. The tax never did. TTB still collects its great-great-grandchild today, $13.50 a proof gallon, hat tipped, no hard feelings.

II

Is America drinking less?

TTB's operational reports track what the country actually makes. Beer removals fell 21 percent from 2015 to 2025, and wine production 31 percent. These figures run on calendar years, not the fiscal years the collections use; the firearms tax below is the exception, shown by fiscal year.

III

Tobacco built this tax base. Tobacco is shrinking it.

Tobacco collections fell from $14.5 billion in FY 2015 to $8.9 billion in FY 2025, a $5.5 billion drop that outweighs TTB's whole net decline; rising spirits collections offset part of it. The rate has been $1.01 a pack since 2009, so the fall is volume: taxed cigarettes dropped by more than half after 2012.

IV

The one tax on this page that is spent on wildlife.

The firearms and ammunition excise tax, FAET, is 10 percent of the sale price on pistols and revolvers and 11 percent on other firearms and ammunition, paid by makers and importers. It is TTB's smallest tax, $808 million in FY 2025, and the only earmarked one.

The surge, one round per quarter

Firearms and ammunition excise tax collected each fiscal quarter, FY 2015 through FY 2025. Each cartridge stands as tall as that quarter's collection.

Source: TTB Statistical Release TTB S 5630, Tax Collections, final annual cumulative summaries, FY 2015 to FY 2025, quarterly detail. FY 2020's third and fourth quarters reflect COVID-era filing postponements that shifted collections into the fourth quarter.

The fine print

Returns are quarterly on TTB Form 5300.26, and since a 2010 law ended semimonthly deposits, payment is due with the return. Sales purchased with Department of Defense or Coast Guard funds are exempt, and since October 2005 anyone who makes or imports fewer than 50 firearms in a calendar year owes no tax on them.

V

Volumes fell. So why do permits keep climbing?

A TTB permit is the price of entry, and entry boomed: registered breweries up sixfold since 2010, distillery permits up more than fivefold since 2012, bonded wine producers roughly doubled.

Permit counts run higher than producer counts, and both are right.

Why?

Permits count every premises authorized to operate; the production charts count only those that filed reports that year. In 2024: 15,166 registered breweries, 7,888 reporting. The permit files also cover only the continental United States.

Who's behind the bureau notices?

TTB

The Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau runs one of the best returns in government. In FY 2025 it served more than 132,000 authorized industry members and processed nearly 163,000 label applications with 502 employees and $157.8 million in budget authority, and every program dollar spent on collection brought $199 back. The house rules for a release

STR-01The working file carries the report's expected columns, with the published header names.
STR-02Every row names a state and a reporting period.
STR-03One row per state per period. Duplicates double-count the national total.
VAL-01Production, removals, and stocks cannot be negative.
VAL-02Opening stocks plus production should roughly cover removals plus closing stocks. A large residual suggests a keying or unit error.
VAL-03A state more than 40 percent from the same period last year gets a review, not an automatic correction.
DIS-01A cell reflecting fewer than 3 reporting brewers is reviewed for aggregation into Other states, so no single taxpayer can be inferred (26 U.S.C. 6103).

The fine print, all of it
  • Tax collections come from TTB Statistical Release TTB S 5630, Tax Collections, using each fiscal year's final annual cumulative summary, FY 2015 to FY 2025, from ttb.gov/taxes/tax-audit/tax-collections. TTB labels these releases unofficial; official revenue figures appear in the TTB annual report. In plain terms, the statistical release is the bureau's running tally and the audited annual report is the official record; this page uses the statistical releases and says so. Domestic figures come from TTB's tax database by return period end date, and import figures come from U.S. Customs and Border Protection data.
  • Production and removals come from the TTB National Statistical Reports for beer (TTB S 5130), wine (TTB S 5120), and distilled spirits (TTB S 5110), published as yearly open data files covering calendar years 2012 to 2025, from the statistics pages at ttb.gov/data. TTB revises these as amended returns arrive; figures here reflect the files as pulled in July 2026.
  • Tobacco removals come from the TTB tobacco statistics yearly open data file, calendar years 2012 to 2025, using the taxable removals series, which includes removals from Puerto Rico. Cigar detail for 2013 is redacted in the source file.
  • Agency figures (industry members, label applications, staffing, budget, revenue per program dollar) come from the TTB Annual Reports for FY 2019 to FY 2023 and the FY 2025 TTB Annual Financial Report. TTB published no FY 2024 annual report. Where TTB published only rounded figures, this page repeats TTB's rounding.
  • Permit and registration counts come from TTB National Revenue Center count-by-state spreadsheets for breweries and bonded wine producers (continental United States) and the TTB Distilled Spirits Permit Counts report (beverage alcohol permits only).
  • Years differ by dataset. Tax collections use the federal fiscal year, October through September. Production, removals, and permit counts use calendar years.
  • Units. A barrel of beer is 31 gallons. Wine is measured in wine gallons. Spirits are measured in proof gallons, one liquid gallon at 50 percent alcohol by volume. Dollar figures are nominal, not adjusted for inflation.
  • What "taxable removals" means. Production is what a premises made during the year. Taxable removals and withdrawals are what left bonded premises with federal excise tax paid, which is the volume entering the taxed market. Exports and industrial-use removals leave tax free and are excluded from the taxable figures.
  • Suppression. Where publishing a national total would reveal proprietary data, TTB redacts it. The 2025 total for spirits taxable withdrawals is redacted and appears as a gap in the chart. The gap is deliberate: no redacted value on this page was estimated or imputed.
  • Firearms figures use each fiscal year's own final S 5630 release. TTB adjusts prior-year figures in later releases as unclassified collections are classified (FY 2018 FAET appears as $620.4 million in its own final report and $624.7 million in the FY 2019 report, for example), so small differences from later publications are expected. FAET facts (rates, filing, exemptions, the Pittman-Robertson earmark) come from TTB's FAET Reference Guide and Tax Rates pages.
  • Agency history (the 1934 Alcohol Tax Unit, the 1935 FAA Act, ATF in 1972, and TTB's creation on January 24, 2003 under the Homeland Security Act of 2002) comes from TTB's own history pages: The TTB Story, the FAA Act historical background, and the FAET background page.
  • Historical tax rates come from TTB's Historical Tax Rates page (series through 2017) and the current Tax Rates page (2018 to present). The prologue chart shows general rates only; the historical page lists beer entries like "$18/$7" without defining the second figure, so reduced small producer rates appear in the text only. Wine's lower tax class boundary changed in 2018 from 14 percent alcohol to 16 percent under the Craft Beverage Modernization Act, so the wine series splices two slightly different class definitions at that point.
  • Small lines not shown. Special occupational tax (about $250 thousand per year) and unclassified collections are included in totals but too small to chart.
  • The back room. The latest beer counts and the three-month chart use Beer Institute monthly taxable removals estimates compiled from TTB data: the March, April, and May 2026 reports, published May 5, June 9, and July 7, 2026. The prior-year comparison figures in those reports are TTB-reported. TTB's official monthly reports post two to three months after the period and supersede the estimates as each month lands. The 2026 monthlies are now posting: the distilled spirits report dated June 26, 2026 covers January through March 2026 and redacts taxable removals with a dash where a figure could identify a particular taxpayer, a gap this page keeps as a gap. The filing due dates in the dossier come from TTB's operational-report due dates pages and the regulations they cite, 27 CFR 25.297 for beer and 27 CFR 24.300 for wine. The working file in the check demonstration is synthetic, written for this page, because real Brewer's Report of Operations data is confidential until released in aggregate.
  • Known limits. Dollars are nominal, not adjusted for inflation. Collections figures come from statistical releases TTB labels unofficial. Brewery and bonded-wine counts cover the continental United States only. TTB publishes no volume by brand or company, so none appears here. Within those limits, every figure is exactly as TTB published it.

Where this money goes

Alcohol and tobacco collections flow to the general fund. FAET does not: under the Pittman-Robertson Act of 1937, all of it is earmarked for wildlife restoration, held in trust for the states.

Where did the 19 percent go?

Tobacco did the falling. Cigarettes leaving for the taxed market more than halved after 2012, and the rate has not moved off $1.01 a pack since 2009, so tobacco collections dropped $5.5 billion between FY 2015 and FY 2025. That one product lost more than the $4.9 billion the house shed on net; rising spirits collections poured part of it back. Fewer smokers, not softer rates.

Figures from TTB Statistical Release TTB S 5630, final annual summaries, FY 2015 and FY 2025.
